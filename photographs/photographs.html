Set-up Notes for Digitizing Photographic Prints

Last updated 10/18


Camera and Lighting Set-up:

  • Ideally, best to shoot photographic prints on the IQ3 100MP on Bay 4 TTI (using box & vacuum board) or the P65 on Bay 5 (using vacuum board)

  • Double check that the camera is level to the shooting surface (the vacuum board on top of box)

  • Using 4 light setup if on Bay 4 (2, of course, on Bay 5)

    • Less raking light is often better for most photographic prints, especially those with a textured surface

  • Using black velvet shoot-through foam core (to mitigate any reflections and to attain richer shadow details)

    • Verify that lights are not too high that they imped even illumination

      • If you can look up from the level of the print and see the full extent of the lights, on each side, you are good to go

  • Base the orientation of the digital back on ppi aims

    • 800 ppi for the IQ3

    • 600 ppi for the P65

  • Rotate the digital back when needed to mitigate highlight issues, i.e., curved edges and hard, pronounced folds

  • Aperture will most often fall between f8 and f8.5 for optimal image quality

  • Use Trello notes to easily reference set-up specifics for duration of your project, for example:

    • IQ3 100MP on Bay 4 TTI (4 light set-up)

    • Aperture: f8

    • Pack power: 7.2

    • Light angle: 20 degrees (or 70 degrees, depending on orientation of angle finder)

    • Light height: 83" (to pin)

    • < lights >

      • Lights up to wall

      • Lights centered to lens

    • Camera column height: 89 cm

    • Resolution: Approx. 850 ppi


Safe Handling:

  • Use nitrile gloves as recommended by Conservation


Shooting:

  • Double-check that the sequence of photographs matches that of the work order using various means:

    • Confirm the entire contents of a box first

    • Confirm in batches

    • Confirm one image at a time

  • Possibly take a number of prints out of their sleeves first, for rhythm of shooting

    • Do whatever is most comfortable for your style of shooting, checking focus and sequence

  • Verso orientation shall match that of the recto

  • Make a capture and check your 5-points of focus, using focus aids and/or 8.5 x 11‚Äù book capture target

    • One trick is to use the verso for focus refinement given the texture and printed material that is present

  • Once dialed in, check focus every now and again

  • Decide whether peeling corners can be coaxed to lay flat in order to mitigate hot spots

    • Use a gloved finger, a bone folder, a piece of paper, etc.

    • As mentioned above, back rotation can help resolve these reflections too

  • Use caution with folded corners. Perhaps best to leave alone.

  • When a project is primarily of uniform size, but there are outliers, use cut pieces of matching background paper to fill in any void within the mat window when necessary

  • Hard folds that catch a highlight may require a placeholder in both the session and the box

    • Return to such prints at the end of the session with the back now rotated vertically

      • Check focus and create a new LCC

  • Once vacuum is on use compressed air for each print

    • Give more attention to dusting shadow areas and darker tones
